# AI Proficiency Evaluation: Concerns & Recommended Approach

**Status:** ⚠️ Active Concerns — Messaging needs revision  
**Key Date:** August 15, 2026 (Board checkpoint)  
**Owner:** Curtis (VP Engineering), CJ Singh (CTO)  
**Last Updated:** 2026-06-23

---

## Executive Summary

The Jarvis AI proficiency initiative has significant messaging problems that are creating anxiety across engineering teams. While the **intent is good** (help people level up, target training effectively), the **execution feels like performance management** despite explicit statements to the contrary.

**Core Problem:** Saying "this is not a performance review" while simultaneously:
- Distributing evaluation rubrics
- Requesting manager assessments
- Mentioning "action plans" and "development plans"
- Setting aggressive deadlines (August 15)
- Delivered by HR (Sandy)

**Root Cause:** CJ's board commitment to 60% AI productivity improvement is driving artificial urgency. August 15 is a reporting checkpoint, not a deadline for consequences — but that's not how it's landing.

**Recommendation:** Reframe entirely as support/skill development opportunity. Be transparent about board commitment. Remove performance management language. Acknowledge urgency while providing realistic timeline for actual learning.

## Background: What Was Communicated

### The Official Message (from HR/Sandy presentation)

**What was said:**
- "This is not a performance conversation"
- "This is not a performance review type of thing"
- "We're just interested in how people are doing"
- "We want to help everyone upskill and adapt to AI"

**What was also said:**
- Fill out evaluation spreadsheet
- Here's the rubric to evaluate people against (Level 1-5)
- People falling behind will have "action plans" or "development plans"
- August 15 deadline for assessment completion
- Managers must assess each person's AI proficiency level
- Self-evaluation + manager evaluation to identify gaps

**Delivery context:**
- Presented by Sandy (HR change management specialist)
- Focus on "change management" and "helping people through transition"
- ADKAR model references (Awareness, Desire, Knowledge, Ability, Reinforcement)

### How It Actually Landed

**Engineer perception:**
> "Everything we're saying about it is that this IS a performance review... What's happening here?"

**Anxiety triggers:**
1. **HR delivery** → Feels official/serious
2. **Evaluation spreadsheets** → Feels like performance management
3. **"Development plans"** → Has specific HR meaning (usually precedes PIP)
4. **August 15 deadline** → Arbitrary and aggressive
5. **Level requirements** → "Will I get fired if I'm not level 3?"

**Questions engineers are asking:**
- "Does this mean layoffs are coming August 15?"
- "If we don't hit level 3, what happens?"
- "Are we doing performance improvement plans?"
- "Is my job at risk?"
- "Why the sudden urgency?"

## Problems with Current Approach

### 1. Mixed Messaging (Credibility Gap)

**The contradiction:**
- Say: "Not performance management"
- Do: All the hallmarks of performance management

**Why this fails:**
- Destroys trust
- Engineers "read between the lines"
- Creates cynicism about leadership communication
- Makes future messages suspect

**Marten's observation:**
> "I'm sitting there like... it doesn't feel like very honest messaging. And that is definitely feedback I've gotten from Daniel who had heard it from other people."

### 2. Unrealistic Timeline

**The math:**
- August 15 = less than 2 months away
- Everyone has a day job (full-time work)
- Maybe 1-2 hours per day for learning
- Expectation: Reach "level 3" proficiency

**Reality check:**
> "What do you learn in two months? If you don't have a job you go to college — what do you learn in two months? You make some progress, it's like half of the next algebra course."

**The setup:**
- Feels like: "In two months you're taking the AI final and if you don't score level 3, we're not telling you what happens but..."
- Creates panic, not productive learning
- Undermines the "we're here to help" message

### 3. Misaligned Expectations

**The performance paradox:**
> "We want to perform at a level of Anthropic or Google. Well, does that mean we're going to pay people like Anthropic and Google too? Are we going to give them the same incentive package?"

**The logic:**
- If we truly succeed in leveling people up to Google/Anthropic standards...
- They would be "fools not to go work at Google"
- Where they'd get better compensation, benefits, equity, etc.

**The disconnect:**
- Asking for elite performance without elite compensation
- Not inherently wrong, but needs acknowledgment
- "We're asking you to be competitive with top companies" (more realistic framing)

### 4. Evaluation Optics

**The spreadsheet problem:**
- Managers filling out evaluation forms
- Rubrics with levels
- Assessment of each person
- Comparing self-eval to manager-eval

**Why this feels like performance management:**
- It literally is the same process/format
- Used in annual reviews
- Has direct consequences historically
- Can't say "this isn't that" when it looks identical

### 5. HR Delivery Context

**Perception problem:**
- Sandy (HR) delivering the message
- Reinforces "this is official/serious"
- Even though Sandy has change management expertise
- Context: "Everything from HR is about performance/consequences"

**The reality:**
- Sandy brought in for process management
- Has tools for managing through change
- But optics matter

## Recommended Messaging Revision

### 1. Lead with Transparency

**Say this explicitly:**
> "Here's what happened: CJ made a commitment to the board that we're going to increase AI productivity by 60% because he wants to show that we're leaning into AI and making progress. That's what's driving the urgency. Nothing else."

**Why this works:**
- Names the elephant in the room
- Removes speculation
- Provides logical explanation for timeline
- Doesn't "throw CTO under the bus" — frames as strategic commitment
- Engineers respect honesty over spin

### 2. Reframe as Support, Not Evaluation

**Current framing:** "We need to evaluate where you are"  
**Better framing:** "Tell us what support you need"

**Message structure:**
> "We want to help you develop AI skills. Tell us what's missing:
> - Don't know where to start?
> - Need access to tools?
> - Want pairing/mentorship?
> - Unclear what 'good' looks like?
> - No time in sprint for learning?
>
> We're trying to give you the stepping stones and remove blockers."

**Key difference:**
- Puts engineer in control
- Frames as opportunity, not judgment
- Surfaces real barriers (time, tools, knowledge)
- Action-oriented ("what help do you need?")

### 3. Acknowledge Industry Reality

**The normalization message:**
> "If you go to any large tech company today, AI proficiency is part of:
> - Hiring criteria (you'll get asked AI questions in interviews)
> - Performance evaluations (it's a column in the rubric now)
> - Career ladders (expectations at each level)
> - Skill expectations (like system design, programming languages, etc.)
>
> We're doing the same thing at GHX. This is now one more column. That should be expected."

**Why this works:**
- Removes the "why is GHX doing this?" question
- It's not GHX-specific, it's industry-wide
- Individuals should feel urgency for their own careers
- Normalizes the change

### 4. Be Honest About Timeline Pressure

**The acknowledgment:**
> "Are we rushing this a bit? Yes. Probably doing this faster than we'd like. Would we prefer organic adoption over 6-12 months? Sure. But here's the reality: the world is changing super fast, every company is doing this right now, and we don't want to fall behind. Both as a company AND as individuals, there's legitimate urgency here."

**Why this works:**
- Admits the obvious (people already feel the rush)
- Explains the 'why' (strategic + career development)
- Validates that urgency is appropriate (not manufactured)
- Frames as "we're in this together"

### 5. Remove Performance Management Language

**Stop saying:**
- "Action plans"
- "Development plans"
- "Falling behind"
- "Assessment" (use "conversation" or "check-in")
- "Evaluation" (use "understanding where you are")

**Start saying:**
- "Targeted support"
- "Focused training"
- "Identifying gaps together"
- "Figuring out what help you need"
- "Understanding where to invest learning time"

**Example reframe:**
> "If your self-assessment says level 3 but your manager sees level 1, that's not a problem — it's useful information. Maybe you're doing things your manager doesn't see. Maybe there's a skill area you need help with. Either way, we can have a useful conversation about it."

### 6. Clarify What August 15 Actually Means

**Current perception:** Deadline for being level 3 or else  
**Reality:** Board reporting checkpoint

**What to say:**
> "August 15 is when we report to the board on adoption progress. It's not a deadline for you to 'be done' or consequences happen. It's a milestone for us to show trajectory. The actual work of skilling up and improving productivity will continue well into Q1 2027 and beyond."

**Why this works:**
- Removes the "or else" fear
- Clarifies it's about organizational reporting, not individual consequences
- Sets realistic expectations (this is ongoing, not a 2-month sprint)

### 7. Focus on the Real Goal

**What actually matters:**
> "The real goal is throughput and cycle time improvement. Six months from now, nobody's going to care about whether you were assessed at level 2 or level 3 in August. The work will speak for itself. We're trying to help you build skills that make your work faster, easier, and more impactful."

**Why this works:**
- Shifts focus from assessment to outcomes
- Removes fear about "the number"
- Emphasizes practical benefits (your work gets easier)
- Aligns with what engineers actually care about (shipping)

- Tools (access to Jarvis, Cursor, etc.)

## What Success Looks Like

### Short Term (August 15)

**Metrics for board:**
- X% of engineers actively using AI tools (Jarvis, Copilot, etc.)
- Y% completed foundational training
- Z pilot projects using AI in meaningful ways

**Reality check:**
- These are adoption metrics, not proficiency certifications
- Show trajectory, not completion
- Emphasize cultural shift starting, not finished

### Medium Term (Q1 2027)

**Observable changes:**
- Throughput improvement (cycle time data)
- Developer satisfaction (AI tools making work easier)
- Quality indicators (fewer bugs, better designs)
- Innovation (teams proposing AI-augmented solutions)

**Actual goal (Curtis):**
> "At the end of the day, the big numbers we're trying to change is throughput and cycle time. That's what we're trying to change."

### Long Term (Ongoing)

**Cultural shift:**
- AI proficiency normalized (like knowing Git, Jira, etc.)
- Part of hiring/career ladder (but not overweighted)
- Continuous learning mindset (not one-time training)
- Teams autonomously adopting AI approaches

**Anti-pattern to avoid:**
- Don't make this a "flavor of the month" initiative
- Don't let it become checkbox compliance
- Don't lose sight of actual productivity improvements
- Don't create AI proficiency theater

## Open Questions & Risks

### Questions to Address

1. **What if someone genuinely can't/won't adopt AI?**
   - Is this truly mandatory?
   - What's the accommodation path?
   - When does it become a performance issue (if ever)?

2. **How do we measure actual proficiency vs. usage?**
   - Level 3 means what, exactly?
   - How do we validate assessments are consistent?
   - Risk of "teaching to the test"

3. **What's the training/support investment?**
   - Time in sprint for learning?
   - Budget for tools/platforms?
   - Access to expert mentorship?
   - Structured training paths?

4. **How does this intersect with performance reviews?**
   - Will AI proficiency be in 2027 reviews?
   - How weighted vs. other skills?
   - Is "met expectations" possible without AI adoption?

### Known Risks

**If messaging doesn't change:**
- Mass anxiety, productivity drop
- Trust erosion in leadership communication
- Resume updating, potential attrition
- Cynicism about "this is optional" statements

**If timeline doesn't flex:**
- Checkbox compliance (people fake proficiency)
- No real skill development
- Missed opportunity for actual productivity gains
- Disappointment when August numbers don't match expectations

**If transparency doesn't improve:**
- Rumor mill fills vacuum
- Worst-case assumptions spread
- Managers field questions they can't answer
- Morale damage
