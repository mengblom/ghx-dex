# Management by Artifact: Pattern Analysis

**Status:** Private strategic analysis
**Date:** June 8, 2026
**Purpose:** Understanding the deeper management philosophy at play

## The Observed Pattern

### Core Philosophy
CJ appears to operate on a principle: **If people are required to produce artifacts (evidence of their work), it ensures they do what they're supposed to do.**

### Mechanism
- Request deliverables (presentation decks, OKR updates, metrics reviews)
- May not actually consume the material deeply
- The production itself is the forcing function
- "Spin the wheel" random reviews create ambient pressure
- Everyone must always be ready to present

### Evidence
- Direct statement (2nd hand): CJ explicitly said this is part of how he manages
- Observable pattern: Deliverables requested but not deeply engaged with
- DORA metrics "spin the wheel" proposal fits this pattern exactly

---

## Why This Matters for DORA

The DORA metrics review proposal isn't just about whether these metrics corrupt. **It's about using metrics as a compliance forcing function.**

### The Logic Chain
1. Require metrics reviews in leadership forums
2. Use "spin the wheel" to randomly call on teams
3. Ambient threat of being called on → everyone always prepared
4. Preparation = every manager constantly managing to metrics
5. Teams know leadership watches → optimize for appearance
6. Result: Chronic metric grooming, exactly what corrupts DORA

### Why This Guarantees Corruption
**The forcing function approach ensures the worst possible DORA implementation:**
- Not occasional metric review → Constant metric readiness
- Not learning-focused → Performance/appearance-focused
- Not team self-diagnosis → External evaluation pressure
- Not discrete events → Ambient surveillance

**This is Goodhart's Law by design.**

---

## The Broader Pattern: Management Theater

### What It Creates

**1. Optimization for Theater Over Substance**
- Time spent making things look good vs. making things be good
- Packaging becomes more important than actual work
- Best presenters succeed, not best performers

**2. Trust Erosion**
- When people realize artifacts aren't deeply consumed, feels like checkbox management
- Infantilizes leaders - assumes they won't work without surveillance
- Message: "We don't trust you to do your job"

**3. Perverse Incentives**
- Manage for the performance review, not for outcomes
- Keep leadership happy vs. keep customers happy
- Artifact production becomes the work

**4. Doesn't Scale with Senior Talent**
- Experienced leaders know how to manage teams
- Surveillance-as-accountability is for junior managers
- Top talent leaves environments that treat them like children

**5. Creates Compliance Culture, Not Performance Culture**
- Focus on evidence of work vs. results of work
- Defensive documentation vs. value creation
- CYA behavior becomes rational response

---

## Why This Philosophy Exists

### The Logic (Charitable Interpretation)
- Some people won't do the work without accountability
- Requiring artifacts forces discipline
- Random checks keep everyone honest
- Transparency through consistent reporting

### When It Works
- **Junior teams** - Need structure and forcing functions
- **Low-trust environments** - Previous bad actors required surveillance
- **Compliance-heavy domains** - Artifacts actually matter (regulatory)
- **Short-term fixes** - Quick way to impose discipline

### Why It Fails at Scale
- **Doesn't differentiate** - Treats all leaders the same
- **Optimizes for wrong things** - Compliance vs. outcomes
- **Expensive** - Time spent on artifacts, not value
- **Drives out top talent** - Best people won't tolerate it
- **Creates what it fears** - Theater and gaming

## The DORA Case as Microcosm

The DORA metrics review proposal is the perfect test case for this philosophy:

### If Intent is Learning (What DORA Designed For)
- Teams use metrics to diagnose their own processes

- Metrics stay useful because no performance pressure

### If Intent is Forcing Function (Artifact Production)
- "Spin the wheel" ensures everyone always ready
- Preparation means constant metric management
- Teams optimize for metric appearance
- Metrics corrupt because performance pressure

**The DORA case reveals which philosophy we're operating under.**

**Upside:**

**Downside:**
- Direct challenge to CJ's core beliefs

**Best if:**

- Other leaders share concern

**How to frame it:**

*"I want to be direct about something I've observed that concerns me about how we approach accountability. I've noticed a pattern where we ask leaders to produce artifacts that may not be deeply consumed for decision-making, but serve as forcing functions - the idea being that requiring the artifact ensures people do the underlying work.*

*I understand the logic, but I think it backfires in important ways:*

1. *It optimizes for theater over substance - people spend time making things look good rather than making things be good*
2. *It erodes trust - when people realize artifacts won't be deeply engaged with, it feels like checkbox management*
3. *It creates perverse incentives - especially with metrics like DORA, where ambient pressure to always be ready drives exactly the gaming we're trying to avoid*
4. *It doesn't scale with senior talent - we're hiring experienced leaders who know how to manage. Surveillance-as-accountability sends the message we don't trust that*

*What I'd rather see:*
- *Review actual outcomes (what teams delivered, customer impact)*
- *Trust leaders to use the right process metrics for their teams*
- *When we ask for artifacts, engage with them meaningfully*
- *Create accountability through results, not through artifact production*

*I worry that 'spin the wheel' reviews feel like we're building a compliance culture, not a performance culture."*
