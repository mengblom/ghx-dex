# Hiring Principles and Philosophy

*Last Updated: 2026-04-17*
*Status: 🌿 Growing*
*Confidence: ✓ Validated*

## Overview

Philosophy for recruiting and talent assessment at GHX, emphasizing potential over perfection, T-shaped capabilities, and modern engineering practices. Core belief: optimize for learning ability and growth trajectory rather than exact credential matches.

## Core Hiring Philosophy

### Optimize for Potential, Not Perfection

**Key Principle**: Rather than seeking the "perfect" candidate who checks every box, prioritize candidates with strong foundational skills, demonstrated learning ability, growth trajectory, and adjacent experiences that transfer to the role.

**Why This Matters**:
- Perfect-match candidates are rare and expensive
- High-potential candidates grow into the role and often exceed expectations
- Focusing on potential expands the talent pool significantly
- Creates a learning culture rather than a "hire experts only" culture

**In Practice**: Don't be afraid to hire someone at the bottom of the band or even one level down if they demonstrate strong fundamentals and growth potential.

### Job Descriptions as Guides, Not Checklists

**Problem**: Organization is immature in how we look at roles and talent. Too often treating JDs as must-have checklists - binary thinking where candidates either perfectly match or don't. Missing the nuance of potential, adjacent skills, and growth capacity.

**Better Approach**:
- JDs are guides to inform search and evaluation, not rigid scorecards
- Focus on candidate's overall fit, potential, and ability to effectively do the role after some onboarding
- Look for capability patterns, not exact keyword matches
- Consider adjacent experiences and transferable skills
- Assess potential and learning ability, not just current skills

**TA Partnership**: TA should help translate intent and partner on finding right candidates, not enforce rigid criteria. Need flexible interpretation of requirements based on market reality.

## Key Attributes to Prioritize

### T-Shaped Engineers

**What We Want**: People with deep expertise in one or two areas (the vertical bar of the T), but enough breadth to collaborate across the stack and contribute beyond their core specialty (the horizontal bar).

**Why This Matters**: 
- Can dive deep when needed, but also collaborate broadly
- Can fill gaps and unblock teammates
- Supports autonomous team operation

**Philosophy**: Generalist mindset with specialist skills.

### AI Experience and Mindset

**What to Look For**:
- Hands-on experience with AI-assisted development tools (Claude, GitHub Copilot, etc.)
- Demonstrated interest through personal projects if not yet using professionally
- Has opinions on how to leverage AI tooling effectively
- Understands AI capabilities and limitations

**Why This Matters**:
- Aligns with AI Native Development vision
- Can help team adopt and improve AI practices
- Critical for staying competitive in modern development

**At Minimum**: Demonstrated interest and curiosity about AI tools, even if not extensive hands-on experience yet.

### Growth and Learning Orientation

**What to Look For**:
- Demonstrates continuous learning
- Adapts to new technologies and approaches
- Shows evidence of skill development over time
- Comfortable with ambiguity and change
- Can learn domain knowledge on the job

**During Interviews**:
- Ask about how candidates learn and adapt
- Explore their experience with AI tools in depth
- Look for evidence of taking on challenges outside comfort zone
- Assess problem-solving approach over memorized knowledge

### Modern SDLC and Architecture Experience

**For All Engineers**: 
- Familiar with agile/scrum methodologies
- CI/CD practices and frequent deployment cadences
- Experience with automated testing and quality practices

**For Senior ICs and Managers**: 
- Experience with distributed, cloud-native, microservices architectures
- Operational patterns: messaging/queuing, observability, deployment strategies
- Infrastructure and IaC (Infrastructure as Code) experience

**Why Cloud and IaC Matter**: 
- Cloud-native architectures require teams to manage their own infrastructure
- Product teams need infrastructure capability for true autonomy
- Can't have centralized infrastructure team own everything (creates bottleneck)
- DevOps skills distributed across teams, not centralized

### Product Thinking

**What to Look For**: Engineers who care about the problem space, the customer, and the end product - not just the code.

**How to Validate**: Ask about their current domain and gauge depth of understanding of the company they currently work for. Look for curiosity about business context and customer problems.

### Team and Culture Fit

**Why It Matters**: Building for the long haul. Culture fit and ability to contribute positively to team dynamics are just as important as technical skills.

**What This Means**: Collaboration skills, communication style, values alignment, ability to work in autonomous team structure.

## Interview Process Philosophy

### Evaluating Beyond the Checklist

**Interviewing Maturity Needed**:
- Ask probing questions about how candidates think
- Explore problem-solving approaches
- Understand their learning and growth trajectory
- Assess cultural and team fit beyond technical skills
- Look for patterns and potential, not perfect matches

**Current Reality**: Organization is quite inexperienced at interviewing and judging candidates holistically. Need to build this muscle.

### Independent Assessment

**Process**: Submit feedback promptly after interviews without coordinating with other interviewers beforehand. We want independent assessment, not group consensus.

**Why**: Preserves individual perspective and reduces groupthink in evaluation.

### Speed Matters

**Philosophy**: Great candidates move fast and have multiple options. We need to evaluate quickly and make timely decisions.

**Implications**: 
- Streamlined, repeatable process
- Prompt feedback submission
- Clear decision-making forums

## Trade-offs to Accept

Explicit trade-offs we're willing to make to optimize for potential:

- Hire someone with strong backend + willingness to learn frontend vs. waiting for full-stack expert
- Hire someone with cloud experience in AWS who can learn GCP vs. holding out for GCP expert  
- Hire someone who has used Claude/GitHub Copilot actively vs. AI-skeptic expert
- Hire someone at bottom of band with growth potential vs. holding out for top-of-band perfect match

## When to Use This

- When writing or reviewing job descriptions
- When screening resumes and deciding who to interview
- When conducting interviews and evaluating candidates
- When making hire/no-hire decisions
- When coaching interviewers on evaluation approach
- When partnering with TA on search strategy

## Alternatives Considered

**Option A: Strict Checklist Approach** - Require all JD criteria to be met
- Cons: Misses high-potential candidates, small talent pool, long time-to-hire
- Why we didn't choose: Optimizes for false precision over actual fit

**Option B: Potential-Focused Approach** (What we chose) - Prioritize learning ability and growth
- Pros: Larger talent pool, builds learning culture, often better long-term outcomes
- Trade-offs: Requires more onboarding investment, slightly higher initial risk
- Why this fits: Aligns with autonomous teams and growth culture; supports faster hiring
