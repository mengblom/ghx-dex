# Product-Engineering Collaboration

*Last Updated: 2026-04-17*
*Status: 🌱 Seedling*
*Confidence: ✓ Validated*

## Overview

Philosophy and principles for Product-Engineering partnership at GHX, emphasizing shared accountability for commitments, clear role boundaries, and joint planning. Core belief: Engineering should not commit delivery dates independently outside of collaborative Product-Engineering planning.

## Core Principles

### Commitment Philosophy

**Key Principle**: Engineering should not independently commit delivery dates outside of joint Product and Engineering planning.

**Why This Matters**: For work not yet fully prioritized, refined, and understood, detailed effort estimates and date certainty would be artificial. Committing without proper scoping creates false confidence.

### Role Clarity

**Product's Role:**
- Weigh priorities across competing initiatives from different stakeholders
- Make prioritization decisions
- Own the "what" and "when" at a strategic level

**Engineering's Role:**
- Assess feasibility
- Estimate effort
- Identify dependencies and sequencing
- Provide delivery confidence levels
- Own the "how" and technical approach

### Forecast vs. Commitment

**Critical Distinction**: We can forecast Q2 at a roadmap level, but we should be careful not to confuse a forecast with a hard commitment.

**Requirements for Valid Commitment**:

For Engineering to commit to delivering work (e.g., "12 defect remedies in Q2"), the following must be true:

1. **Work is properly scoped and estimated by Engineering**
   - Technical approach understood
   - Dependencies identified
   - Effort estimated with reasonable confidence

2. **Work is prioritized highly enough by Product**
   - Against other competing work
   - With understanding of trade-offs
   - With stakeholder alignment

3. **Joint Product-Engineering agreement**
   - Both functions aligned on feasibility
   - Both understand the commitment level
   - Both accountable for delivery

### Anti-Pattern to Avoid

**Scenario**: Stakeholder asks Product for commitment → Product says they don't know capacity → Stakeholder asks Engineering directly

**Why This Fails**:
- Bypasses Product's prioritization role
- Engineering commits without knowing if work will actually be prioritized
- Creates conflicting commitments when priorities shift
- Undermines Product-Engineering partnership

**Better Approach**: 
- Engineering provides feasibility/effort assessment
- Product makes prioritization decision
- Joint commitment emerges from collaboration
- Both functions stay aligned throughout

## When to Use This

- When stakeholders request delivery commitments from Engineering
- When planning quarterly roadmaps or OKRs
- When establishing boundaries between Product and Engineering roles
- When resolving conflicts about who owns delivery timelines
- When coaching team members on stakeholder communication

## Key Phrases to Use

- "We can provide an estimate, but Product needs to confirm prioritization"
- "Let's forecast this jointly rather than commit independently"  
- "Our role is to assess feasibility; Product's role is to prioritize"
- "This requires joint planning to turn a forecast into a commitment"
